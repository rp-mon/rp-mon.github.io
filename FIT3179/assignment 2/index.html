Analysis of global data

In the Choropleth map above, we can clearly observe the countries that have a high and low cancer incidence rate. These observations are accentuated by the fact that the visualisation's scale is on a log-basis, and we can immediately infer that countries such as Australia, New Zealand, USA and Canada have disproportionately high incidence rates, whereas countries in Africa have low rates.

We can explore these effects on a more granular level through the graph on the left, which showcases the supposed relationship between how developed a country is and its cancer rate. Through navigating the options, we can observe a significantly positive relationship between development (on a HDI and classification basis) and cancer rates; this phenomenon can be explained by 'relaxed natural selection', in which modern medicine enables people to survive cancers and allows one's genetic background to be passed from one generation to the next. These effects are most pronounced when comparing African nations to European nations, with the former having characteristics of low development and the latter having characteristics of high development.

Further, some continent-based discrepancies can be explained by factors such as greenhouse gas emission and its effect on skin cancer. This factor is most pronounced in nations within Oceania.
